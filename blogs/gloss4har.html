Can a team of LLMs clean up messy activity labels?

A walkthrough of GLOSS4HAR — a multi-agent LLM system that triangulates passive sensing data with low-effort self-reports to correct annotations and rebuild activity timelines.

IMWUT '26 multi-agent LLM activity recognition doi ↗

Ha Le, Akshat Choube, Varun Mishra, Stephen Intille · Northeastern University

// the problem

Human activity recognition needs labeled data, but labels are painful to get. Participants' self-reports drift — they misremember start and end times, bunch several activities into one, or forget a quick errand. Researchers then spend hours reconciling those reports against sensor streams by hand. In the ACAI dataset this work builds on, 22.6% of the minutes needed correcting.

// the idea

GLOSS4HAR treats annotation cleanup as a sensemaking problem. A network of eight LLM agents plans what evidence to gather, pulls the relevant sensor streams (steps, heart rate, phone use, ambient sound, GPS), reasons over them, and emits a structured, corrected timeline — with a written justification for every change. It does two jobs:

  • Correction — take a participant's annotation and fix bunching, omissions, and boundary errors using the sensors.
  • Reconstruction — rebuild a detailed timeline from a low-effort self-report (a rough activity list) plus the sensors.

// what this shows

The multi-agent structure matters. Against a single-shot RAG baseline, GLOSS4HAR made far more correct fixes per day (up to ~126 min vs ~58) while keeping incorrect fixes low, and its written justifications let a researcher accept or reject each change. On reconstruction, a rough list of activities with approximate times was enough to rebuild timelines that align with researcher-reviewed ground truth — temporal precision, the most tedious thing to self-report, mattered least.
