Can a team of LLMs support low-effort data collection for longitudinal research studies?

IMWUT '26 doi ↗ pdf ↗ code ↗

Ha Le, Akshat Choube, Varun Mishra, Stephen Intille · Northeastern University

// the problem

Collecting high-quality labels for human activity recognition (HAR) in free-living settings is one of the hardest problems in ubiquitous computing. Most studies lean on participants' self-reports, which are often incomplete and temporally inaccurate, and take real effort from both participants and researchers to clean and verify.

As wearables become part of everyday life, that process won't scale. We need annotation methods that improve data quality and the user experience — without researchers manually reviewing every timeline.

// the idea

Humans and AI have complementary strengths.

People remember the meaningful events of their day — studying for an exam, a meeting, a concert, cooking dinner — the high-level activities that are often impossible to infer from sensors alone. But they are unreliable on exact timestamps, short transitions, and brief activities.

Sensing systems are the opposite: they continuously detect movement, flag anomalies, and reason across many data streams, yet lack the personal context to know what those signals mean.

GLOSS4HAR combines the two — a multi-agent LLM system that mimics human sensemaking. Rather than replacing participants, it collaborates with them, triangulating passive sensing with lightweight self-reports to produce more accurate activity timelines.

// research questions

Our work explores two questions:

RQ1 · correcting self-reports

Can a multi-agent LLM identify mistakes in participants' self-reports and suggest reasonable corrections?

Can the system detect omissions, temporal misalignment, and merged activities by combining multiple sensor streams with participants' own descriptions?

RQ2 · low-effort self-reporting

Can a multi-agent LLM support low-effort self-reporting?

Can passive sensing combined with lightweight self-reports — μEMA, activity lists, or end-of-day summaries — recover detailed activity timelines while reducing participant burden?

Throughout the week the phone and smartwatch log passive signals continuously, while participants add two kinds of self-report: quick spoken μEMA check-ins every 15 minutes, and a longer end-of-day session labeling activities on a five-minute grid. Those raw labels still carry recall errors, so the research team reconciled each one against the sensors to build a reviewed ground truth — a process that changed 22.6% of all minutes. That reviewed set is what every model is scored against.

In the correction task, the system sees one participant annotation at a time — its text and timestamp — together with every sensor stream except the held-out thigh accelerometer. It judges whether the label is wrong, proposes a corrected version, and writes a short justification a researcher can accept or reject. We measure how close those corrections land to the researcher-reviewed labels, with a retrieval-augmented (RAG) system as the baseline.

In the reconstruction task the participant never produces a detailed timeline at all. Instead they give a lightweight report, and GLOSS4HAR rebuilds the full hour-by-hour timeline by fusing that report with the passive sensing. We score the result against three references: the thigh-sensor postures, the researcher-reviewed labels, and the participant's own original annotations.

Instead of making detailed, minute-by-minute annotations all day, participants can self-report their activities in much lighter ways — for example:

These formats trade detail for effort — μEMA is fast but sparse, activity lists capture what happened while blurring exact timing, and a spoken narration is the most natural but least structured. Studying all of them in a real deployment would be impractical, so we recreate each format from the detailed annotations we already collected.

Each step throws away a little more information: we drop posture labels and turn exact times into rough durations, jitter those durations, and shuffle some of the ordering to mimic imperfect recall. Dropping the durations entirely yields an untimed list, while passing the timed list through an LLM produces the free-form narration.

// results

Our experiments show strong performance on both tasks.

+7.7 F1
Task 1 · correction. Gain in annotation F1 from GLOSS4HAR's corrections (gpt-4o) over the participant's raw labels — also ahead of a RAG baseline.
86.7% F1
Task 2 · reconstruction. Timeline rebuilt from nothing but a rough activity list plus passive sensing (gpt-4o) — close to what a detailed recall would give.

// takeaway

First, GLOSS4HAR shows the feasibility of an automated human–AI collaboration system that supports low-effort reporting of behavioral data. Instead of asking participants to produce detailed, minute-by-minute annotations, it meets them where they are — reconciling lightweight self-reports with passive sensing — a step away from traditional, high-burden data-collection protocols.

Second, it points beyond the usual homogeneous pipeline of data → model → prediction. A system like GLOSS4HAR can take in many kinds of input at once — not just fixed-format, high-frequency wearable signals, but also high-level contextual descriptions and user feedback — and reason across them toward a prediction. That flexibility is hard to reach with traditional ML pipelines, and opens the door to more adaptive, human-in-the-loop sensing systems.
